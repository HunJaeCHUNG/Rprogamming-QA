How to declare vector in R

One easy way to declare vector in R is to use ‘c()’ commend. Here is a sample code for declaration of a vector \[ a=\left(\begin{array}{c} 1\\ 2\\ 3 \end{array}\right) \]

a <- c(1,2,3)
a
## [1] 1 2 3

There are many opinion about the meaning of ‘c’ here. Some people argue that ‘c’ stands for ‘combine’ while the other people insist that it stands for ‘concatenate’.(http://stackoverflow.com/questions/11488820/why-use-c-to-define-vector)

Each argue has its own rational but for me, I would say it is more convinent for me to memorize that it stands for ‘colum’ since it gives us a colum vector. To check the result of the code is a colum vector, we can confirm its transpose as follows:

t(a)
##      [,1] [,2] [,3]
## [1,]    1    2    3

As we see that the transpose of a is a row vector, thus a itself is a colum vector. It is good to know this fact since it sometimes gives you a totally different calculation result. For example, depending on the order of matrix calculation, it could be a matrix or a number. \[ a^{t}a=\left(\begin{array}{ccc} 1 & 2 & 3\end{array}\right)\left(\begin{array}{c} 1\\ 2\\ 3 \end{array}\right) \] The above dot product of vector a can be written as R code as follows:

t(a) %*% a
##      [,1]
## [1,]   14

However, if we switch the order, the result will be a 3-by-3 matrix as follows: \[ aa^{t}=\left(\begin{array}{c} 1\\ 2\\ 3 \end{array}\right)\left(\begin{array}{ccc} 1 & 2 & 3\end{array}\right) \]

a %*% t(a)
##      [,1] [,2] [,3]
## [1,]    1    2    3
## [2,]    2    4    6
## [3,]    3    6    9

Consider the optimization problem

\[ \begin{array}{cc} minimize & f_{0}\left(x_{1},x_{2}\right)\\ subject\,to & 2x_{1}+x_{2}\geq1\\ & x_{1}+3x_{2}\geq1\\ & x_{1}\geq0,\,x_{2}\geq0. \end{array} \] For each of the following objective functions, find a solution of the problem. The point where the two lines are crossed is \(\left(\frac{2}{5},\frac{1}{5}\right)\).

In a Boolean linear program, the variable \(x\) is constrained to have components equal to zero or one:

\[ \begin{array}{cc} minimize & c^{T}x\\ subject\,to & Ax\preceq b\\ & x_{i}\in\left\{ 0,1\right\} ,\,i=1,...,n. \end{array} \] In general, such problems are very difficult to solve, even though the feasible set is finite (containing at most \(2^{n}\) points). In a general method called relaxation, the constraint that be zero or one is replaced with the linear inequalities : \[ \begin{array}{cc} minimize & c^{T}x\\ subject\,to & Ax\preceq b\\ & 0\leq x_{i}\leq1,\,i=1,...,n. \end{array} \] We refer to this problem as the LP relaxation of the Boolean LP. The LP relaxation is far easier to solve than the original Boolean LP.

This exercise builds on exercises 4.15 and 5.13 in Convex Optimization(textbook), which involve the Boolean LP \[ \begin{array}{cc} minimize & c^{T}x\\ subject\,to & Ax\preceq b\\ & x_{i}\in\left\{ 0,1\right\} ,\,i=1,...,n. \end{array} \] with optimal value \(p^{\star}\). Let \(x^{rlx}\) be a solution of the LP relaxation \[ \begin{array}{cc} minimize & c^{T}x\\ subject\,to & Ax\preceq b\\ & 0\preceq x\preceq1. \end{array} \] so \(L=c^{T}x^{rlx}\) is a lower bound on \(p^{\star}\). The relaxed solution \(x^{rlx}\) can also be used to guess a Boolean point \(\hat{x}\), by rounding its entries, based on a threshold \(t\in\left[0,1\right]\): \[ \hat{x}_{i}=\begin{cases} 1 & x_{i}^{rlx}\geq t\\ 0 & o.w., \end{cases} \] for \(i=1,...,n\).

Evidently \(\hat{x}\) is Boolean (i.e., has entries in ${ 0,1} $). If it is feasible for the Boolean LP, i.e., if \(A\hat{x}\preceq b\), then it can be considered a guess at a good, if not optimal, point for the Boolean LP. Its objective value, \(U=c^{T}\hat{x}\), is an upper bound on \(p^{\star}\). If \(U\) and \(L\) are close, then \(\hat{x}\) is nearly optimal; specifically, \(\hat{x}\) cannot be more than \(\left(U-L\right)\)-suboptimal for the Boolean LP. This rounding need not work; indeed, it can happen that for all threshold values, is infeasible. But for some problem instances, it can work well. Of course, there are many variations on this simple scheme for (possibly) constructing a feasible, good point from \(x^{rlx}\).

Now, we will find out the best approximation of the optimal point of LP. To find it, we need to calculate the upper bound \(U=c^{T}\hat{x}\) first. Carry out threshold rounding for \(100\) values of \(t\), uniformly spaced over \(\left[0,1\right]\). For each value of \(t\), calculate \(\hat{x}\) from above and keep the the objective value \(c^{T}\hat{x}\) and the maximum constraint violation \(max\left(A\hat{x}-b\right)\). The best approximation of the optimal point of the LP can be obtained by finding the \(\hat{x}\) which makes the gap of \(\left(U-L\right)\) to be a minimum.

The following code is used for finding \(\hat{x}\):

t <- rep(seq(0, 1, length.out = 100),100)
t <- t(matrix(t, nrow = 100, ncol = 100))
optp <- matrix(optp, nrow = 100, ncol = 100)
x_hat <- optp - t
x_hat[x_hat>=0] <- 1
x_hat[!x_hat>=0] <- 0
